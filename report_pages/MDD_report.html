DIAGNOSIS AND TREATMENT OF MDD (DEPRESSION):

CHALLENGES, PROGRESS, AND PATIENT REALITIES

Understanding Major Depressive Disorder

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD, or depression) is a pervasive mental health condition affecting around 30 million people in Europe alone, marked by significant disability and, in severe cases, increased mortality due to suicide. MDD extends far beyond feeling sad or low; it deeply disrupts daily functioning, relationships, and overall well-being. Despite numerous available treatments, MDD remains a challenging condition for doctors, patients, and families.

Diagnosing MDD: The Multifaceted Challenge

Diagnosis of MDD involves a thorough evaluation of symptoms such as persistent low mood, loss of interest, changes in appetite, sleep disturbances, fatigue, and thoughts of worthlessness or suicide. For many, physical symptoms blend with overwhelming feelings of isolation and loneliness, making it difficult for patients to communicate their struggles, even to close friends or family. Patients we've spoken to have told us that their common challenges with MDD are primarily about the combination of physical symptoms with feelings of isolation and loneliness.

Standardized questionnaires and clinical interviews help establish a diagnosis, though some patients underestimate the emotional effort required to honestly assess their condition. Completing these assessments can be taxing, often surfacing uncomfortable or distressing thoughts, with patients telling us that they would need to mentally prepare for these diagnosis questions, calls, or appointments.

Key barriers to effective diagnosis and engagement often include the following:
  • Under-communication of symptoms, particularly insomnia and cognitive issues
  • Difficulty navigating medical terminology and questionnaire processes
  • Emotional toll and stigma associated with admitting mental health challenges

Treatment Landscape: Options, Satisfaction, and Unmet Needs

MDD management is complex, due to the varied and individual nature of depression. There are numerous treatments, including several classes of antidepressants, adjunct therapies, and more recently, novel agents trained at treatment-resistant cases. Despite this wide range of interventions, both patients and healthcare professionals acknowledge moderate satisfaction with outcomes, telling us that their current satisfaction with MDD treatments is moderate - there are many treatment options, but tolerability and efficacy needs to improve.

Key challenges facing current treatments include the following:
  • Slow onset of action. Many antidepressants require 3-8 weeks for noticeable effect, prolonging patient suffering.
  • Partial or non-response. Around 30% of MDD patients do not adequately respond to at least two antidepressant trials, earning the diagnosis of “treatment-resistant depression” (TRD).
  • Side effects and tolerability. Weight gain, sedation, sexual dysfunction, and metabolic disturbances are common and can reduce adherence.
  • Burden of trial-and-error. Patients may spend years navigating through ineffective treatments.
